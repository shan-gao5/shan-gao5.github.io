PhD: Why?

This question came up in almost every interview.

I never had a good answer.

There is probably an admission-friendly version. It says something about intellectual independence, long-term research goals, and wanting to work on fundamental questions in statistical learning theory or AI safety. It is not false. I do want those things. But its never really the truth and in the moment it always felt strange to say it as if it were.

So I always gave a dishonest answer. For one specific school though, I felt like I needed to be honest, and maybe it killed my chances.

I said that I had a hunch I would enjoy it. I said that I liked my current research and wanted the chance to explore more topics. I said that I wanted to develop a research style I could call my own.

Even though it’s partly true, I was always slightly embarrassed by that answer.

There is a certain story we tell about research. In this story, the researcher is moved by a private, almost inhuman curiosity. They would think about the same problems on a desert island. They would do the work even if nobody were watching, even if nobody cared, even if there were no conferences, no collaborators, no late-night office conversations, no coffee after a talk, no supervisor telling them that the thing they just said is either very interesting or obviously wrong.

I have never recognized myself in that story.

When I started undergrad, I did have something like that dream. I wanted to understand something fundamental. I wanted to find a field and go deep enough into it that the world became sharper.

But for a while, nothing did that to me. Nothing really caught my attention that way. I could still work. I could still read papers, prove small things, run experiments, talk about ideas. But there was no private fire burning in the background. Even though the problems always fought back , no problem really followed me home and demanded that I think about it. I stopped dreaming (?)

What kept me going was not pure curiosity. It was people.

It was the long meetings with my supervisor, where we would talk about research and then, somehow, for even longer about life, politics and making fun of other people. It was my colleagues and friends, who had the same strange envy toward fun problems: the desire not only to solve them, but to be the kind of person who could solve them. It was the small community that had carried me since first year. It was the feeling that research, at its best, was not a solitary act of genius but a shared way of paying attention.

I have received much more from that world than I have given back.

That is hard to admit without turning it into a performance of gratitude. But I am where I am because people were generous with me. They gave me time, advice, patience, arguments, references, opportunities, and friendship.

I feel like there is a debt in that. Not the kind anyone will collect. In fact, I know that if I do well, most of these people will consider everything settled. It's the most unfair thing about mentorship and friendship: the people who give the most often ask for the least precise repayment.

Still, I feel a bit of burden from it.

And maybe part of wanting to do a PhD is wanting to become the kind of person who can repay it indirectly. Not by balancing the books, but by joining the same chain. By becoming a better collaborator. By taking ideas seriously. By being generous with someone else when it is my turn.

But it is hard to keep going without genuine curiosity. It is hard to continue on obligation alone. It is hard to know whether wanting to stay near the people who made you is the same as wanting the work itself..

That was the part I could not say cleanly in interviews.

Professors often seem to want the desert-island answer. They want to hear that, stripped of everything else, you would still be thinking about the same problems. Maybe some people would. Maybe that is real for them. But it never made much sense to me.

On a desert island, I would probably not do research.

I would miss the people too much.

Suppose I woke up tomorrow and discovered that I was the last person left in the world. Would I still care about research? Would I still sit at a blackboard, rewrite the same proof sketch ten times, look for the one simple modification that makes the argument go through? Would I still feel that an interesting problem was interesting?

Probably not
