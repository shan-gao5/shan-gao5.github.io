Life,Career, Academia In review

2026-05-22 · stories

In the final week of the winter semester, my supervisor told me that I was almost done with my thesis. There was only a short computation left to verify the assumptions of the saddle point method. I then started doubting myself:

I wanted to be a great scientist as a kid.

I wanted to discover something fundamental about the world and maybe that'll tell me something about my own existence.

Physics

This pushed me into studying Physics in my first year of University. Then I did a summer of high-energy physics research. It was less physical intuition than writing code. It was a lot of python/mathematica programming in a time that predated chatGPT. I had great teammates and a really cool mentor Matteo. I published my first paper!

The physics department at McGill was filled with some genuinely good people. It was a close knit group and everybody was rooting for each other. It was probably the most humane experience I had a McGill. I remember every single person I interacted with. I hope to meet them again someday. Most of them aren't in Montreal anymore.

In the end, I switched out of Physics because I was scared, but I completely regret it, it would've been really cool to stick with it and maybe join an experimental physics lab.

Mathematics

I switched out of Physics into Mathematics, because I took an ODEs course and I thought it was pretty cool.

A lot it seemed invented, but we were learning about the building blocks of so many tools that we use today, and that was cool enough to keep me in it.

In the end, the topic that interested me the most about math was probability theory which is, coincidentally, the only math course I didn't score an A in (maybe that's what pushed me towards it).

I was pretty confident in my conviction to study math until I had a conversation with Professor Prakash Panangaden. Where he called me out on my learning style.

He told me to give him the definition of uncountable (cardinality strictly greater than $\mathbb{N}$ ), and then prove that $\mathbb{R}$ is uncountable. Pretty easy, I learnt that in my first math course. I wrote it down in about a minute.

"You don't see the vastness in it"

the uncountability of the reals, and it's proof through Cantor's diagonal argument, is simple. It shows that any supposed list of all real numbers can be defeated by constructing a new number that differs from the $n$-th listed number in its $n$-th digit, and it is beautiful because this tiny trick reveals that infinity comes in different sizes. Taking this one step further, in computer science, Cantor’s clever diagonal might also cut across the endless list of programs and, in the margin, write a function no machine on the list can ever become, such as the halting problem!

In hindsight, I sort of get him and I'm sort of jealous of his experience. I understood the proof before I felt the meaning. It became a tool to solve assignment questions, but it was a revelation for him.

I'm immensely thankful for him. Not only because of how he challenged my way of seeing and approaching mathematics, but because even though I was completely the opposite of what he envisioned a good academic to be, even though I was completely stumbling on my words and answer to some advanced questions he asked, he still believed in me and wanted to take me in as a student (this sort of healed me from previous failures to secure research positions).

It's unfortunate he went into poor health, I wish him the best retirement. I hope I can one day convey to him how much that conversation meant for me.

Computer Science

I had no plans of taking computer science courses. But adding that major to my program, in my days, meant that I could go to school for free.

In one semester, I took the holy trinity of algorithm courses: COMP 553 (algorithmic game theory), COMP 362 (algorithm design) and COMP 252 (algorithms and data structures). I liked it, and I wanted to do research in it.

Professor Hamed Hatami, and Professor Pooya Hatami took me in, to do statistical learning theory research. I remember one cold day I walked into his office and I just asked him straight up to work with him and he accepted! I'm immensely grateful for these two and my good friend Ari Blondal.

During that summer, I learnt that Hypergraph Ramsey Theory is actually useful and became an expert in applying Concentration inequalities in funky ways.

I got introduced to the mathematical analysis of machine learning algorithms. Specifically, to PAC learning, making learning algorithms cool and private through Differential Privacy.

I wish I worked harder during that summer. I would get demotivated when I got stuck, but I should've talked more to Hamed, who was always willing to spend time to help me.

Then I graduated, and with no direction for the future, my current supervisor, professor Luc Devroye took me in while I figured something out. That year turned out to be, maybe, the most enjoyable year of my life, and I don't know why. It's probably the camaraderie I had in the lab every day. I loved talking to my labmates everyday. I enjoyed talking to Luc a lot, who always made time for us to chat about math, life, politics and life.

Exit?

Now I look for jobs in software development. I actually started coding when I was 13 and always enjoyed it. I didn't go into it because I wanted to do something different from my dad Bo Gao. In hindsight, I should've done the same thing and went to Waterloo, where I had an offer to study computer engineering. I have no doubt I would've been a great embedded systems software engineer :)

I remember building things before was a super tedious process, but now with the advancements of artificial intelligence, this is actually quite fun and learning new tech stacks/software is easier than ever, so I might stick with this for a while.

It sucks that I haven't been getting that many interview opportunities because my CV is full of academic stuff, but I hope I can find something cool soon!
